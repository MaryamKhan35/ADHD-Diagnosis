ADHD Brainwave Patterns

Research shows that individuals with ADHD often exhibit distinct brainwave patterns, including increased theta activity and decreased beta activity, particularly in frontal regions.

  • Elevated theta/beta ratio in frontal cortex
  • Reduced beta power during attention tasks
  • Atypical alpha wave modulation
  • Altered connectivity between brain regions

Question 1: Which brainwave frequency is most associated with attention and focus?

A) Delta waves (0.5-4 Hz)
B) Theta waves (4-8 Hz)
C) Beta waves (13-30 Hz)
D) Alpha waves (8-13 Hz)

Correct! Beta waves (13-30 Hz) are associated with active thinking, focus, and alertness. Individuals with ADHD often show reduced beta activity during attention tasks, which contributes to attention difficulties.

Question 2: What is the typical theta/beta ratio pattern in individuals with ADHD?

A) Elevated theta/beta ratio
B) Reduced theta/beta ratio
C) Normal theta/beta ratio
D) No consistent pattern

Correct! Individuals with ADHD typically show an elevated theta/beta ratio, particularly in frontal brain regions. This pattern reflects increased slow-wave (theta) activity and decreased fast-wave (beta) activity during attention tasks.

Question 3: Which neurofeedback protocol is commonly used for ADHD treatment?

A) Alpha suppression training
B) Theta/beta ratio training
C) Delta enhancement training
D) Gamma blocking training

Correct! Theta/beta ratio training is the most common neurofeedback protocol for ADHD. It aims to decrease theta activity while increasing beta activity, thereby normalizing the atypical brainwave patterns associated with ADHD.

ADHD Myths vs Facts

Click on each card to reveal the truth behind common misconceptions about ADHD.

MYTH

ADHD is just a lack of willpower or laziness

FACT

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental disorder with biological basis. It's not about willpower but about differences in brain structure and function that affect attention, impulse control, and executive function.

MYTH

Only children can have ADHD

FACT

ADHD often continues into adulthood. About 60% of children with ADHD continue to have symptoms as adults. Adult ADHD may present differently but still significantly impacts daily functioning.

MYTH

People with ADHD can't focus at all

FACT

People with ADHD can focus intensely on tasks they find interesting (hyperfocus). The challenge is regulating attention - starting, sustaining, and shifting focus when needed.

MYTH

Medication is the only effective treatment

FACT

While medication can be helpful, comprehensive treatment often includes behavioral therapy, coaching, lifestyle changes, and educational support. Many people benefit from combined approaches.

MYTH

ADHD is overdiagnosed and not a real medical condition

FACT

ADHD is a well-established neurodevelopmental disorder with decades of research support. Brain imaging studies show structural and functional differences in ADHD brains.

MYTH

People with ADHD are less intelligent

FACT

ADHD affects people across all intelligence levels. Many individuals with ADHD are highly intelligent and creative. The disorder affects executive function, not intelligence itself.
